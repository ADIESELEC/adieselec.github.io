What Is a Certificate of Compliance (COC) — And Why You Need One

If you're buying, selling, or insuring a property in South Africa, you've probably come across the term "COC." It's one of those documents nobody thinks about until they urgently need it — usually right in the middle of a property sale.

What a COC actually is

A Certificate of Compliance is a legal document confirming that a property's electrical installation meets the safety standards set out in the Occupational Health and Safety Act (specifically the Electrical Installation Regulations). It can only be issued by a registered person — a qualified electrician registered with the Department of Employment and Labour.

When you legally need one

  • Selling a property — a valid COC (issued within the last two years) is a legal requirement for property transfer. Conveyancers will not proceed without it.
  • After any electrical work — any new circuit, addition, or alteration to the electrical installation requires an updated COC.
  • Insurance claims — many insurers require a valid COC to process claims involving fire or electrical damage. Without one, a claim can be reduced or denied.
  • Renting out a property — while not always a strict legal requirement, many landlords and letting agents require one for liability protection.

What the inspection actually covers

A COC inspection checks the full electrical installation — wiring condition, earthing and bonding, the distribution board, circuit breakers, and any additions made since the last certificate. If anything doesn't meet current standards, it needs to be corrected before the certificate can be issued.

How long is a COC valid?

There's no fixed legal expiry, but most transactions (property sales in particular) require a certificate issued within the last two years. Practically, a COC stays valid until something changes in the installation — a new plug point, an added circuit, or an alteration means a new certificate is needed.

Common mistakes homeowners make

  • Assuming an old COC is still valid after DIY additions or changes
  • Leaving COC arrangements until the last minute during a property sale, causing delays
  • Using an unregistered electrician — a COC issued by someone not registered with the Department of Labour isn't legally valid

If you're planning to sell, insure, or simply want peace of mind that your home's wiring is actually safe, getting a COC inspection sorted early avoids last-minute stress.
