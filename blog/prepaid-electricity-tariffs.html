Prepaid Electricity in South Africa: Tariffs Explained

If you're on prepaid electricity, you've probably noticed your units disappear faster some months than others — even with similar usage. That's usually down to how South African prepaid tariffs are actually structured, not a fault with your meter.

How prepaid tariffs actually work

Most municipalities (and Eskom direct customers) use an Inclining Block Tariff (IBT) system. This means the price per kWh increases as you use more electricity in a billing cycle — it's not a flat rate. The first "block" of units is cheapest, and each subsequent block costs more per unit.

  • Block 1 — the cheapest units, usually covers basic low-usage needs
  • Block 2 & 3 — progressively more expensive per unit as usage increases
  • Higher blocks — significantly pricier, meant to discourage high consumption

This is why buying electricity in smaller, frequent top-ups instead of one large purchase can sometimes cost you more overall — you can end up straight into a higher block sooner in the cycle.

Fixed charges to watch for

Depending on your municipality, part of every prepaid purchase may go toward a fixed network or service charge before any units are added. This varies by area — City of Cape Town's structure differs from Eskom direct-supply areas, so it's worth checking your specific municipal tariff schedule.

Practical ways to get more out of your prepaid spend

  • Spread large appliance use (geysers, pool pumps) across off-peak times where your tariff allows
  • Fit a geyser timer — geysers are often the single biggest home electricity cost
  • Check your DB board for old, inefficient circuits that may be drawing more than they should
  • Consider solar or a hybrid inverter setup to offset daytime usage, especially if you work from home

If your bill still feels high after accounting for tariff structure, it's worth having your wiring and major appliances checked — inefficient old wiring, a failing geyser element, or an outdated DB board can quietly inflate usage without any obvious warning signs.
