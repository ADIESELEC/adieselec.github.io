Is Your Solar System Actually Doing What It Should?

Load shedding pushed a lot of Cape Town homeowners into installing inverters, batteries, and solar panels quickly — sometimes without a full understanding of what "good" actually looks like. A system can be installed, switched on, and technically working, while still leaving you in the dark exactly when you need it most.

Here's how to check whether your setup is genuinely doing its job.

1. Does it actually carry your critical loads through a full outage?

Many systems are sized for lights and a router, not a fridge, geyser, or WiFi for the full duration of a stage 4-6 outage. If your inverter cuts out or your battery drains before the power comes back, the system is undersized for what you actually need it to do.

2. Is your battery actually reaching full charge before load shedding starts?

If your charging settings, panel angle, or panel size aren't matched to your usage pattern, your battery might be sitting at 60-70% when the outage hits — meaning you're running on half a tank without knowing it.

3. Are your changeover times fast enough?

A good system should switch from grid to backup power seamlessly. If your lights flicker, your router drops out, or your computer restarts every time the power goes, your changeover settings likely need adjusting.

4. Common signs something's off

  • Your battery is regularly depleted before grid power returns
  • Certain appliances (geysers, pool pumps) were never actually wired into the backup circuit
  • You're not sure what your system's actual runtime is under full load
  • The system was installed years ago and settings haven't been reviewed since

None of these mean the system is broken — most of the time it just means the settings, sizing, or wiring weren't matched properly to how the household actually uses power. A short inspection can usually identify exactly where the gap is.
